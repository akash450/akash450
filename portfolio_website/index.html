Website Link

CoronaTracker

This website aims to educate users about the progress of the coronavirus pandemic. It alerts the user on the safety level of the destination in NSW they are planning to visit. This website achieves these goals by presenting data in a visual, minimalistic interface and allow users to make easy, informed decisions about the places they are travelling so they may keep themselves and the ones they love safe. Once a location has been searched, a safety level indicator as well as some general safety tips/suggestions will appear at the bottom. Also displayed are the daily count of cases and total cumulative cases and deaths in NSW. Users can also view graphs of likely sources of infection and affected age ranges. Additional features include personalised twitter feeds, a share button and a day/night toggle to keep users interested.

Deliverables

Changes to Concept

Responding to feedback from Part B, the group got together and brainstormed some ideas to improve our design and aesthetics. Firstly, we added an information button as the prior feedback suggested that people were not finding the website as intuitive as we wanted it to be since there were no indicators on how to use the website. They remarked that the website was not easy to navigate due to this reason. However, we did not want to stray away from the minimalistic approach, hence we decided to add an information button that could be clicked to reveal the site's purpose and instructions on how to use the website.

Another key change we made was with general aesthetics. As the feedback suggested, we chose a predominantly white colour scheme so as to get rid of the dull atmosphere that the earlier iteration showed with the light gray colour scheme. The colour scheme in the night mode was unchanged. We also decided that the layout of the site should be more linear so that the information was easier to follow. The graphs, originally supposed to be to the right of the map, were moved down for two reasons: the aforementioned reason and because, upon original implementation, it decreased the readability of the map.

In addition to the above changes, we made a few changes of our own. An important change was replacing news articles with Twitter feeds. This would give a more personalised touch and create a more "relatable" atmosphere for the target audience. News articles tend to be negative and instill fear in a user. As we want to politely inform the user rather than scare them away, Twitter feeds would be the best choice since they display a more positive side to the pandemic, retaining more users in the process.

Minor changes include refining the safety ranking range and making the website more responsive. We decided to be more specific with the safety ranking as it is not a black and white concept. An area can contain few cases and so it is neither safe nor unsafe. Hence, we expanded the range to include:

  • Safe: 0 - 9 cases
  • Low Chance: 10 - 29 cases
  • Mildly Unsafe: 30 - 59 cases
  • Unsafe: 60 - 99 cases
  • Very Unsafe - 100+ cases

Finally, although we were originally planning to make a desktop-based site, as the most common users would be between the ages of 30 and 40, we decided to make the website more responsive. This is because our target audience also included younger people, who would most likely use this site on a phone or tablet.

Reflections

In retrospect, rather than demonstrating ourselves, I think we should have allowed the users to play with the website themselves; this would have increased the appeal of our website more and attracted more users. Since it was our first time having a virtual tradeshow, the team had no idea how to make our website accessible to other users. Also because we couldn't see how users were. It was definitely a new experience for all of us compared to previous years. I think we could have gained some valuable information about user experience if we allowed users to navigate through our website rather than demonstrating. However, given the tradeshow was online, it was difficult to see exactly what users were experiencing in the first place. Having a virtual tradeshow has its disadvantages but, overall, it was a very fun time for all teams involved! The most fascinating part is it's a great way to view other peoples' work as well! From the feedback we got, I think that our success criteria for simplicity, engagement and informativity were achieved. We did not get to see how easy our website was to navigate for other users but I think this area could be certainly improved. Looking back, most users probably would not look at the website info button, so the website needs to contain more navigational tools. This could be something like popup menus whenever a user is about to hover over an element to perform an action. Users were also looking for a slightly more aesthetic look than what we had in our final website. For example, people gave feedback about using a colour such as blue instead of the white background we had. So we could have forgone a few additional features such as the 3D/2D map toggle to achieve a more aesthetic design. All throughout the lifespan of the project, we had been focusing on functionality, thinking that aesthetics could be easily fixed later. But now I see that aesthetics should have been given equal priority to programming as this can determine how much users "like" to use the website.

Course Reflections

Expectations

I had expected this course to be similar to DECO1400, which I thoroughly enjoyed last semester. Initially, I couldn't clearly visualize what this course was about or what team I would end up with as there weren't any previous reviews on the course. However, I did expect the course to be easy and fun given that you had skilled, supportive team members.

As the semester went on, I found myself with a team that was perhaps not as motivated as they should have been. This may be due to the idea that we picked, as we all had vastly differing ideas and we equated more interactivity with a higher uniqueness factor. I believe, looking back, that we should have picked a topic that we were truly passionate about, no matter if it did not have the interactivity we wanted. We would have worked out the unique features as time went on.

When it comes to team dynamics, I believe I did my best as the leader of Team 6. Initially, I was facing some difficulty as the team communication was shaky and no one had a clear idea of what their roles were. This meant that most of the roles shifted onto me, and so, although I envisioned myself to be a designer on the team, I ended up doing the main development and basic UI design. However, in the final stages after Part B, I realised I was the one who was pushing myself too hard, and decided to rest for the time being. I saw that the rest of my team was more than happy to pull together to create the final design. At this point, we really started to understand each other more, thus evening out the workflow. Seeing the near-finished product, and seeing as how I did all the important work up till that point, I assume my team finally got motivated to finish the website.

Learning Experiences

I had set out some learning expectations in my first journal post:

I hope to advance my coding knowledge in [PHP] further. I also hope to learn about how to use APIs to facilitate front-back end communication.

I got to learn PHP in the workshops, and while it was recommended for a faster performance in the website, because we decided to implement a topic which was heavy on interaction, and because it was more convenient to just use Javascript (there were way more examples on AJAX calls), we went with using Javascript AJAX. So I didn't get to actually implement PHP on my own website. However, in the end, I did get to learn the dynamics of back end systems, through the workshops and through implementing my own site. In my learning, I also discovered great API libraries and frameworks that I can now use for future projects (Leaflet.js, ESRI). In the end, these ended up being some of our core features. I had also wanted to improve my design skills, but unfortunately didn't get much opportunity to do so as I started to become more of a developer. I became very functionality-centred with the site, although I helped make a few design decisions in Part A.

I would like to create a solid team that I can hopefully befriend to develop a strong network of likeminded people who can help each other in times of need.

When it came to group work, I realised an important tenet of working in a team, that is healthy communicaation. I was trying extra hard to be the team leader and to get everything done despite the enormous pressures on me. As I saw after Part B, my team had the skill all along to complete the website, and I realised that I was stressing myself more than required. It was something to do with my leadership of the team. Despite the problems that can arise with teamwork, I wouldn't do this project alone. I really got close to my team members, and they helped me out a lot during the semester. Without their help, I would not have been able to get the site from Part B to final product standard. My team members also seemed to be more design-oriented just like me and brought their novel ideas that would really shape the final aesthetics of the site. I only wish that my teammates were more proactive in their learning rather than just listening to what I had to say.

Regarding course activities, I found the contacts to be very engaging and supportive. We could always rely on a tutor's help whenever we were stuck on implementation. The first contact was especially important as it not only got our creative juices flowing right away, we could gauge what kind of people would be best suited as team members. However, I do believe that preparing for the important contact assessments, such as concept pitches and writing reports, took away from our time actually designing and implementing our main website. I feel like the course could have done with less of the reports and only 1 pitch. It is a bit disappointing when your team has vastly differing ideas, as was my case, and so you have to forgo your initial exploration concept for a topic that the entire team would find easy to implement. I learnt that the topic you chose greatly affected motivation throughout the semester and, hence, the result of your work. I feel that I could have taken more time trying to think of ways to combine our ideas rather than just going with one idea that may not be liked by all team members. For example, one of our team members had an idea for real estate and another for historic pictures of QLD. Maybe, we could have combined these 2 ideas and done a website that was about historic real estate, which would have been perhaps more engaging than a topic such as tracking coronavirus cases, which is naturally gloomy-sounding.

Portfolio

Design Process

This portfolio was my first opportunity to showcase some of my creativity in making a quality website that tells a good story of my journey through this course. With simplicity in mind, I started by drawing a simple wireframe for the layout of the page and some of the navigation options. I also considered responsiveness of the webpage.

I began with the most important part of a portfolio, its basic layout. I created a simple but mobile-friendly HTML skeleton, keeping progressive enhancement in mind, and added content along the way so I could see what I was styling. Then I started thinking of colour schemes to implement in CSS, choosing warm, but pure, solid colours to keep the site simple. I also constantly tested the responsiveness of the website on my phone, as well as in the element inspector of different browsers.

I chose two main plugins: an image carousel plugin and an 'animate-on-scroll' plugin to increase useability, and to enhance the experience of the user by further minimizing website content and . I chose the plugins because they made the website feel more alive and not plain and static. I also chose them as they had the best browser support and responsiveness. I first started designing the site header, which initially contained the 'About Me' section. However, since I wanted to go for a minimalistic design, I chose to have a large header with simple text and an arrow to scroll down. I chose a solid ebony colour to give good contrast and to further simplify the design.

I then designed and styled the dividers between each section for easily distinguishing different sections. The top navigation menu links to each section, and there is a 'Back To Top' link at the bottom of the website to quickly scroll back up.
